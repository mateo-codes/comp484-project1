What are Progressions?

In calisthenics, progressions are step by step ways to make an exercise harder (or easier) while keeping good form. Instead of just adding weight, you can also increase difficulty through leverage, the range of motion, slowing down, and maintaining control.

The goal is to master each step before moving on. Clean reps and full control matter more than rushing to advanced movements. You'll just end up getting hurt and have to stop training while you heal.

How to Progress Safely

  • Control first: If you can't control the movement, it's too advanced.
  • Full range of motion: Use a range you can own with good form.
  • Progress gradually: Add difficulty in small steps. The last thing you want is to get hurt.
  • Quality over quantity: Stop sets before form breaks down.

Push up Progressions (Chest / Triceps)

  1. Incline push ups (hands on bench/couch)
  2. Knee push ups
  3. Standard push ups
  4. Decline push ups (feet elevated)
  5. Diamond push ups
  6. Archer push ups
  7. One arm push ups

Pike / Handstand Progressions (Shoulders & Balance)

  1. Incline pike push ups
  2. Pike push ups
  3. Feet elevated pike push ups
  4. Wall handstand holds
  5. Negative handstand push ups
  6. Wall handstand push ups
  7. Freestanding handstand push ups

Dip Progressions (Triceps / Chest)

  1. Bench dips
  2. Assisted parallel bar dips
  3. Negative dips (focus on slowly lowering)
  4. Full bodyweight dips
  5. Gymnastic ring dips (rings make everything harder for some reason)
  6. Weighted dips (can take these as far as your heart desires)

Pull up Progressions (Back / Biceps)

  1. Dead hangs (grip strength)
  2. Scapular pull ups
  3. Australian rows (bodyweight rows)
  4. Band assisted pull ups
  5. Negative pull ups (slowly lowering)
  6. Full pull ups
  7. Chest to bar pull ups
  8. Archer pull ups
  9. Weighted pull ups (very humbling)
  10. Muscle ups
  11. One arm pull ups (insane)

Squat Progressions (Legs)

  1. Box Squats
  2. Bodyweight squats
  3. Jumping squats (explosiveness)
  4. Split squats
  5. Bulgarian split squats
  6. Hawaiian squats
  7. Pistol squats

Core Progression

  1. Plank
  2. Hollow Body Hold
  3. Hanging Knee Raises
  4. Hanging Leg Raises
  5. Toes to Bar
  6. L-sit hold

Skill Goals

Skills are what make calisthenics both exciting and EXTREMELY frustrating. I would suggest picking one skill to practice 3-4 times per week before your workout for about 30 minutes.

Some examples include

  • Handstands: 80% balance, 20% strength
  • Muscle ups: Explosive pull ups + straight bar dips + transitional drills
  • Front Lever: Need a strong back honey
  • Planche: Can quite literally take years to learn

While learning some of these skills it's important to remember that it can take several years to learn some of these skills. It ultimately depends on how hard and consistently you are at practicing it. While it can be frustrating, it's really important to not get discouraged and learn how to enjoy the process. Calisthenics is quite literally a journey, there's alot more to it than just working out and getting strong. It can teach you how to stay disciplined and what it means to show up for yourself everyday.

On our mindset page we take a deeper look into how calisthenics is not only about functional strength, but also how it can improve other areas of your life outside of the gym. The most important thing of course is to never give up.
